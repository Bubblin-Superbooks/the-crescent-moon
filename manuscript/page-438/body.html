At a third, sheltered by a graceful screen of taller acacias, p. 253they found a bottom on which there was room for both of them to turn. The whistling of the frogs grew so shrill that it hurt their ears. In the middle of the donga no stream flowed; but caught in a series of shelving rock-pools a little of the water of the last rains had lodged. It smelt stale and was cloudy with the larvæ of mosquitoes.

“Now we had better drink,” he said.

“This?”

“Yes. It is not bad water.”

“But I’m not thirsty. And even if I were . . .”

“You must drink it all the same. We must keep the water in our bottles. We shall want that later. Drink as much as you can . . .”
